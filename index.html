The Basics

What Is Cat Social Enrichment?

Social enrichment means giving cats activities and interactions that improve their mental and emotional health. Cats need more than food, water, and a clean kennel.

🐾 What Cats Need

  • Exercise to stay physically healthy
  • Mental stimulation to stay sharp
  • Exploration of their environment
  • Positive human interaction and trust building
  • Opportunities to play and hunt naturally

✨ When Cats Get Enrichment

  • They feel significantly less stressed
  • They stay physically and mentally healthier
  • They show their true personality to adopters
  • They become more adoptable — faster!
  • Even sitting quietly with a cat can be enriching

🔍 Exploring

Safety First

Important Rules & Approved Play Areas

Following these rules keeps all cats safe, healthy, and comfortable during their stay.

🚨

VERY IMPORTANT: Cats Should NEVER Free Roam the Cat Adoption Area

This rule exists for critical reasons. Please read and remember all three:

🚪
Escape Risk
If a door opens while a cat is loose, they could escape the building.
🦠
Disease Prevention
Cats near other kennels can spread germs or illness to other animals.
😰
Stress Reduction
Cats in kennels may become stressed when another cat runs past them.

Before You Begin

When Are Cats Eligible for Free Play?

Not every cat is ready for free play immediately. Cats must meet all of these requirements before participating.

📅
At Least 3 Days in Shelter The cat must have had time to adjust to their new environment. Kittens may sometimes adjust faster.
💊
No Signs of Contagious Illness The cat must be healthy and show no symptoms that could spread to other animals.
🩹
No Recent Surgery (48 hrs) Cats must not have undergone surgery within the last 48 hours for their safety.
😌
Relaxed Body Language The cat should show signs of comfort, not fear or high stress, before free play.
💡 If you ever feel unsure — ask staff before moving a cat!
Body Language

Understanding Cat Body Language

Before interacting with a cat, always watch their body language. We use the Fear, Anxiety, and Stress (FAS) Scale to understand how a cat is feeling.

😻

Green — Relaxed and Happy

This cat is comfortable and open to interaction. Great for play or cuddles!

👁️ Soft, relaxed eyes
🐈 Tail up in a relaxed curve
👂 Ears facing forward
😸 Purring
🚶 Approaching you willingly
🐾 Kneading with paws
✅ What to do: These cats are usually great candidates for play sessions or cuddle time! Let the cat guide the interaction — they'll show you what they enjoy. Begin with offering your hand for a sniff, then follow their lead.
😟

Yellow — Mild Stress

This cat is starting to feel unsure. Slow down and give them more space.

😤 Furrowed brow
👂 Ears turned sideways
🐈 Tail wrapped tight around body
👀 Watching you carefully
💪 Slight body tension
🔄 Pulling away slightly
⚠️ What to do:
→ Slow down your movements
→ Give the cat more personal space
→ Let the cat choose whether to interact — don't force it
→ Consider ending the session and trying again later
😾

Red — High Stress

This cat is scared and needs space immediately. Stop interaction now.

😡 Hissing
🔊 Growling
🐾 Swatting at you
👂 Flattened ears against head
⬇️ Body pressed low to ground
🚪 Attempting to escape
🚨 Stop interaction immediately. Give the cat plenty of time and space to calm down. Do not return until the cat has had a full break and is showing calmer signals. Report to staff if stress signs persist. Never punish or try to "push through" red signals.
Every Cat is Unique

Different Cat Personalities

Just like people, cats all have different personalities and preferences. Always let the cat decide what type of enrichment they want!

🎾 Favorite Toys

  • ✓ Wand toys that mimic prey movement
  • ✓ Toy mice and small stuffed animals
  • ✓ String and feather toys
  • ✓ Balls that roll and bounce
  • ✓ Noisy or textured toys for stimulation

⏱️ Play Session Tips

  • ✓ Keep sessions 10–15 minutes long — cats are sprinters!
  • ✓ Move the toy away from the cat like prey escaping
  • ✓ Change speeds to mimic real prey movement
  • ✓ Hide toys behind objects to spark curiosity
  • ✓ Let the cat catch the toy sometimes
  • ✓ Slow down near the end to help the cat cool down
🏆 Pro Tip: Always allow the toy to "lose" at the end so the cat feels successful and satisfied from the hunt!

💜 What They Love

  • ✓ Gentle petting and physical affection
  • ✓ Brushing sessions
  • ✓ Soft talking and being spoken to
  • ✓ Sitting on laps with a clean towel
  • ✓ Head scratches and chin rubs

😻 Signs They're Enjoying It

  • ✓ Purring loudly and continuously
  • ✓ Leaning into your hand for more
  • ✓ Kneading with their paws (making biscuits!)
  • ✓ Licking your hand as a sign of affection
  • ✓ Slow blinking at you (a cat's "I love you")
🧺 Remember: Always place a clean towel over your lap and shirt when cuddling — one towel per cat, and a fresh one each time!

🫧 What They Prefer

  • ✓ Quiet, calm environments
  • ✓ Very slow, non-threatening movements
  • ✓ Sitting nearby without touching at first
  • ✓ Gentle, soft talking
  • ✓ High-value treats to build trust slowly

🌱 Helping Fearful Cats

  • ✓ Move slowly and deliberately always
  • ✓ Speak softly — calm tones help
  • ✓ Avoid direct eye contact — it can feel threatening
  • ✓ Keep interactions very short — 2 to 3 minutes
  • ✓ Offer treats to gradually build trust over time
⏳ Patience is everything. Fearful cats may take days or even weeks to feel safe — every small step forward matters enormously!
Safe Handling

How to Safely Handle a Cat

Proper handling keeps both you and the cat safe. Always follow these steps when removing a cat from their kennel.

1

Offer Your Finger First

Let the cat sniff your finger through or near the kennel door before attempting to open it. This lets them learn your scent and decide if they're comfortable before you make any moves.

2

Read Their Body Language

Watch for FAS signals carefully. The cat should show relaxed or mildly curious body language before you proceed. If they show yellow or red signals, wait and try again later.

3

Support Their Entire Body

When picking up the cat, support their full weight — including their back legs. Bring them close to your body so they feel secure and stable, not dangling or unsupported.

4

Transport Calmly to Play Area

Move slowly and calmly to the designated play area. Keep your face turned away from the cat's mouth and claws. Stay composed — your calm energy helps the cat feel safe.

🦵

Support the back legs at all times — unsupported legs create panic

🤗

Keep the cat close to your body so they feel secure and grounded

🙅

Never let the cat dangle — this causes fear and injury risk

😶

Keep your face away from the cat's reach for everyone's safety

🧺 Important Hygiene Rule: Cuddle Towels

✓ Place a clean towel over your lap and shirt before cuddling a cat
✓ Use one towel per cat — never share between animals
✓ Use a fresh, clean towel for every individual cat
✓ Sanitize your hands thoroughly between each cat
💡 You may leave the towel in the cat's kennel afterward — your scent provides comfort!
Social Time

Group Play Guidelines

Some cats may play together — but only under specific conditions. Group play requires careful observation and strict rules.

📋 Requirements

✓ Must be 6 months or older
✓ Must be healthy and illness-free
✓ Should be similar age groups
🐱 Juveniles: 6 months – 1 year
🐱 Adults: 1+ years
🐱 Seniors: 10+ years

🏠 Group Size Limits

🌿 Catio: Maximum 4 Cats
🚪 Meet & Greet: Arrived Together Only

Watch introductions closely for the first 5–10 minutes before stepping back to observe from a distance.

👁️ What to Watch For

✓
Normal: Hissing, growling, cautious observation — this is okay and expected.
✗
Stop Play: Swiping or lunging at each other
✗
Stop Play: Locking eyes and freezing — escalation is coming
✗
Stop Play: Any aggressive behavior — calmly remove the newest cat
Sanitation

Cleaning & Sanitation Rules

Cleaning is extremely important in a shelter environment. It prevents the spread of illness between cats. Always clean before AND after each play session.

1

Remove Bedding & Cloth Items

Take out all fabric, bedding, and cloth items from the play space before cleaning.

2

Sweep Away Debris

Sweep away all fur, litter, and debris. Organic material must be fully removed first.

3

Clean All Surfaces

Wipe down all surfaces thoroughly, paying attention to corners and frequently touched areas.

4

Sanitize with Rescue Disinfectant

Apply properly diluted Rescue disinfectant to sanitize the space completely and safely.

⚠️ Critical Rule: NEVER spray disinfectant over organic material like fur, urine, or feces. You must clean debris FIRST — otherwise the disinfectant will not work properly and cats won't be protected.
⏱️

Play Time Limits

Free play sessions should last up to 30 minutes. If a longer session is needed, the room must include litter boxes and water bowls — which must then be removed and sanitized before the next cat uses the room. At the end of your shift, always make sure every single cat is safely back in their kennel.

👀 What to Watch During Play

🏃
Active Stress
Running, swatting, excessive movement
🫥
Passive Stress
Freezing in place or excessive hiding
🙈
Normal Hiding
Brief hiding is okay — excessive is a concern
Quick Reference

Do's & Don'ts

Keep these rules in mind every single time you work with cats. When in doubt, always err on the side of caution.

✅ Always DO

✓ Watch cat body language before and during every interaction
✓ Let the cat choose the type of interaction they want
✓ Keep play sessions short — 10 to 15 minutes maximum
✓ Use clean towels for cuddling — one per cat, always fresh
✓ Sanitize your hands thoroughly between each cat
✓ End sessions calmly and on a positive note
✓ Ask staff whenever you feel unsure about anything

❌ Never DON'T

✗ Let cats roam freely in the cat adoption floor area
✗ Force a cat to interact when they don't want to
✗ Ignore warning signs like hissing, growling, or swatting
✗ Use the same towel between different cats
✗ Skip cleaning procedures — even when you're in a hurry
✗ Overstimulate cats with sessions that are too long
✗ Leave your shift without confirming all cats are kenneled
